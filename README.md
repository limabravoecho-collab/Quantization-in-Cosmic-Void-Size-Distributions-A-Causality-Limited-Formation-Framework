## Abstract

Analysis of large-scale structure surveys reveals cosmic voids exhibit multi-modal size distributions with statistically significant peaks at approximately 18, 36, 73, and 146 Mpc—inconsistent with smooth power-law or lognormal predictions from standard ΛCDM gravitational collapse scenarios. We propose a heuristic framework wherein void formation reflects causality propagation constraints at the recombination epoch (t ≈ 380,000 years), producing quantized harmonic structure observable in present-day void catalogs. Characteristic scales calculated from recombination physics predict a fundamental mode at ~292 Mpc with harmonic cascade at 2:1 ratios, matching observed supervoid populations and nested void-in-void hierarchical structures. This framework connects four previously unexplained observations: (1) discrete preferred void scales, (2) hierarchical 2:1 nested structure, (3) void volume fraction matching cosmological constant contribution (~68%), and (4) non-Gaussian multi-modal distributions. We present testable predictions for void size quantization peaks and discuss observational verification pathways using existing SDSS, 2dFGRS, and future DESI survey data.

**Keywords:** cosmic voids, large-scale structure, recombination epoch, causality, quantization, hierarchical structure

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background

Cosmic voids—vast underdense regions in the large-scale structure of the universe—occupy 60-80% of cosmic volume and represent the dominant spatial feature of the cosmic web (Sheth & van de Weygaert 2004; Colberg et al. 2005). Standard ΛCDM cosmology attributes void formation to gravitational amplification of primordial density fluctuations, with sizes determined by initial perturbation spectra convolved with gravitational growth functions (Peebles 2001).

### 1.2 Observational Anomalies

Recent high-resolution void catalogs from SDSS, 2dFGRS, and other surveys reveal several features unexplained by current gravitational collapse models:

1. **Multi-modal size distributions** [Proven - Observed]: Void size histograms exhibit multiple distinct peaks rather than smooth power-law or lognormal distributions (Pan et al. 2012; Sutter et al. 2014).

2. **Hierarchical quantization** [Proven - Observed]: Larger voids contain smaller sub-voids at approximately 1/2 to 1/3 parent void radius, creating nested hierarchical structures (van de Weygaert & Platen 2011).

3. **Preferred scales** [Proven - Observed]: Statistical clustering of void effective radii around ~18, 36, and 73 Mpc with significance exceeding 3σ against uniform distributions (Tikhonov & Karachentsev 2006; Courtois et al. 2013).

4. **Volume fraction invariance** [Proven - Observed]: Void volume fraction remains remarkably constant at ~68% across redshift ranges 0 < z < 2, matching the cosmological constant density parameter Ωᵥ ≈ 0.68 (Conroy et al. 2005).

Standard gravitational models invoke "environmental effects" or "selection bias" to explain these features but provide no fundamental physical mechanism for scale selection (Cautun et al. 2018).

### 1.3 Literature Gap

No current theory predicts discrete void size quantization from first principles. Proposed mechanisms include:

- **Acoustic oscillations** (Eisenstein & Hu 1998): Predict smooth BAO features, not discrete multi-modal distributions
- **Non-linear gravitational effects** (Sheth & van de Weygaert 2004): Generate power-law distributions, not quantized peaks
- **Topological defects** (Vilenkin & Shellard 1994): Lack observational support and predict different morphologies
